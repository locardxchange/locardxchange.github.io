Havighurst Developmental Tasks (Reflection Question) Total: 20 points

Havighurst's concept on developmental task emphasizes that “When people fail to accomplish developmental tasks at a stage, they're often unhappy and are not accorded the desired approval by society”. Do you agree or disagree with this? Provide personal experiences to support your discussion.

Total: 20 points

I agree with Havighurst’s claim. Developmental tasks are socially patterned expectations that arise in particular life periods, shaped by maturation, sociocultural demands, and personal values; as a result, these “tasks” become public markers of readiness and legitimacy. When a person deviates from what is expected at a stage, the consequences are often internal tension and external disapproval, because society uses these milestones to decide who is “on track” and who is not Havighurst (1953).

My experience at the Philippine Science High School shows how developmental tasks operate as enforced social scripts. In our setting, taking and passing the University of the Philippines College Admission Test (UPCAT) was treated as a near-obligatory task for a college-bound student—less a personal option than a norm tied to identity and prestige. I did not accomplish my UPCAT form at first, and I was the only one who broke that norm. My guidance counselor called me in and pushed me to file it, and I eventually took the UPCAT primarily because of societal pressure. In the same period, I graduated last in rank: 71st out of 71. Ranking is a visible label that people easily treat as a forecast of your future, yet internally I believed I was set for life because I knew I was versatile. That mismatch between institutional evaluation and self-concept created real friction, which is consistent with Havighurst’s point that failing—or refusing—to follow a stage expectation can invite unhappiness and reduced approval Havighurst (1953).

In college, the developmental-task pressure shifted from admission performance to credential completion. I was taking BS Civil Engineering in UP, but I did not care about it; instead, I delved into arts, film, and sports, and I earned early—sometimes twice or thrice what I later earned as an assistant professor in UC. In athletic competitions, art exhibits, and film festivals, I often felt at the center of the spotlight because performance mattered, and recognition was immediate. However, whenever I shared my radical view that I did not need a degree and that education had become too commercialized, I could sense disagreement even in microexpressions. That was a reminder that society protects credential norms as gatekeeping mechanisms for legitimacy. Over time, I realized I could not take down the structure through resistance alone; I had to complete the task the system recognizes. I stealthily transferred to UC and dedicated five years to finish both my undergraduate degree and master’s—society won. Even then, completion did not feel socially sufficient; I pushed to finish my master’s cum laude, and with some luck I fulfilled that expectation—unwillingly, but successfully. This sequence reflects the force of developmental tasks: society does not only impose milestones; it can escalate what counts as acceptable accomplishment, and the desire for approval and legitimacy can shape decisions even when one’s competence is formed beyond formal schooling Havighurst (1953).
